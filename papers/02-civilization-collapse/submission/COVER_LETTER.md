We are pleased to submit our manuscript "Coordination Collapse and Civilizational Decline" for consideration in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*.

## Summary of Contribution

This paper presents the **K-Index framework**, the first unified quantitative theory capable of explaining why complex societies collapse across diverse historical and geographical contexts. Our central finding is that civilizational collapse is fundamentally a **coordination failure** driven by trust erosion below a critical threshold (θ ≈ 0.35-0.40).

## Key Innovations

1. **Unified Mechanism**: We identify trust (H₃) as the "keystone variable" determining societal resilience, integrating insights from Tainter's complexity theory, Diamond's environmental determinism, and Turchin's cliodynamics into a single mathematical framework.

2. **Four Laws of Coordination Collapse**: We formalize collapse dynamics through four empirically-grounded laws governing trust conservation, entropy, asymmetric recovery, and equilibrium attraction.

3. **Comparative Methodology**: Our analysis includes both collapsed civilizations (Bronze Age, Roman, Maya, Soviet) and control cases (Egypt, Byzantium, China, Northern Maya), demonstrating that collapse is not inevitable but contingent on trust maintenance.

4. **Modern Predictive Application**: The framework generates testable predictions for contemporary societies, including concerning trajectories for the United States, Brazil, and the United Kingdom.

5. **Digital Age Extension**: We extend classical collapse theory to address unprecedented challenges from algorithmic amplification, manufactured distrust, and platform-mediated trust networks.

## Significance for PNAS Readership

This work bridges multiple disciplines:

- **Archaeology/Anthropology**: Provides quantitative framework for collapse studies
- **Political Science**: Explains institutional breakdown and democratic backsliding
- **Sociology**: Formalizes social capital dynamics with threshold effects
- **Complex Systems**: Demonstrates cascade dynamics in socio-political networks
- **Policy Science**: Offers actionable early-warning indicators and intervention principles

The timing is particularly relevant given rising polarization, declining institutional trust, and democratic backsliding across established democracies. Our framework provides both diagnostic tools and intervention design principles.

## Methodology Highlights

- **Multi-proxy estimation** of historical variables with explicit uncertainty quantification
- **Sensitivity analysis** demonstrating robustness across reasonable parameter ranges
- **Control case methodology** distinguishing correlation from causation
- **Falsifiable predictions** enabling prospective validation
